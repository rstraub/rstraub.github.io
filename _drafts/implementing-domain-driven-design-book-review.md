*The Red Book*. I held high expectations for this title, did it deliver? Find out in this review.

## 📜 The Red Book

[Implementing Domain-Driven Design by Vaughn Vernon]() is affectionately called *The Red Book* by the Domain-Driven Design (DDD) community, often seen as a valuable companion to the original.

*But what sets it apart from [The Blue Book]()?*

Although it touches on all concepts from the original book, The Red Book introduces *Strategic Patterns* before the *Tactical Patterns* in DDD. It also delves into the concept of *Domain Events*, which didn't make The Blue Book.

Throughout the book the reader follows a fictional case study that helps make the abstract ideas concrete, hence the "Implementing" part of the title. The book ends with an extensive application example showing readers how DDD should manifest itself in code.

## ⚖️ Likes and Dislikes

Bruggetje
My likes and dislikes about the book can be summarised as follows:

| Like | Dislike |
| :---- | :---- |
| Case Study | More "Fluff" |
| Code Samples | Code Samples Age |
| Domain Events / Event Sourcing | Content Similar to Blue Book |
| Emphasis on Strategic Patterns | |
| Concepts fell into place | |

The standout differentiator with The Blue Book, for me at least, are the case study, and code samples. DDD consists of conceptual ideas, which I struggled with after reading the original title. Implementing DDD does a great job of helping you understand them more thoroughly, all the while nuancing options.

A downside to offering code samples is that they age. Examples are dated in places, some using redundant technologies.

The chapters on Domain Events and Event Sourcing can be considered as some of the few true additions to the concepts in The Blue Book. These concepts are great to understand, and make part of your architectural "toolbox".

Another great aspect of this book is how it emphasises the importance of the Strategic over the Tactical Patterns of DDD. This addresses a common issue in practice where teams apply the technical patterns without the overarching Strategic ones. Doing so greatly reduces the effect of DDD.

Lastly, and most importantly to me, the book made the pieces of the puzzle fall into place. More so than The Blue Book, which I found valuable, but at times hard to follow. This in itself made the book worthwhile for me.

## 🙋 Who Should Read It?

Are you new to Domain-Driven Design? Or did you read The Blue Book, and want more examples of the concepts (like me)? 

Look no further, Implementing Domain-Driven Design will deliver.  This title is more approachable than the Domain-Driven Design book, working hard to explain the concepts in a simple way. This made concepts "click" more for me than with The Blue Book.

The adept DDD practitioner can safely pass on this title, except if the chapters on *Domain Events*, *Event Sourcing* or case studies are of particular interest to you.

## ⭐ The Verdict

Implementing Domain-Driven Design, or "The Red Book", is an amazing book for anyone interested in DDD. It has some minor flaws, but on the whole I highly recommend this book to newcomers of DDD, or people who want tangible examples with the abstract concepts of DDD.

Final score: 8 out of 10.
